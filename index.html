üìà Credit Card Customer Attrition Deep Dive

Dataset: Credit Card Customers

üìå Project Context: In my BI role, I frequently analyzed churn data for banking clients. This analysis represents the type of exploratory work I do when a stakeholder notices abnormal churn patterns and asks "why is this happening?" I start with what seems obvious, then dig deeper to find the real drivers.
üìä Discovery: What Makes Customers Stay Longer? ‚ñº

Started with a simple observation from the churn data‚Äîsome customers had more products than others. I wanted to see if this correlated with retention. This type of hypothesis testing is common in our intake meetings when business stakeholders have hunches about what drives behavior. 

-- First pass: Do customers with more products stick around?
SELECT 
    Total_Relationship_Count,
    Attrition_Flag,
    COUNT(*) as customer_count
FROM credit_card_customers
GROUP BY Total_Relationship_Count, Attrition_Flag
ORDER BY Total_Relationship_Count;

‚úì Found a pattern, but needed proper metrics. Calculated actual retention rates to quantify the impact. 

-- Segmented by relationship groups to see the real difference
WITH relationship_groups AS (
    SELECT 
        CASE 
            WHEN Total_Relationship_Count <= 2 THEN 'Low (1-2 products)'
            ELSE 'High (3+ products)'
        END as relationship_tier,
        Attrition_Flag,
        COUNT(*) as count
    FROM credit_card_customers
    GROUP BY relationship_tier, Attrition_Flag
)
SELECT 
    relationship_tier,
    COUNT(*) FILTER (WHERE Attrition_Flag = 'Existing Customer') as retained,
    COUNT(*) as total,
    ROUND(100.0 * COUNT(*) FILTER (WHERE Attrition_Flag = 'Existing Customer') / COUNT(*), 2) as retention_rate
FROM relationship_groups
GROUP BY relationship_tier;
üéØ What I Found:

Customers with 3+ products have 86.86% retention vs 73.11% for those with 1-2 products. That's a 13.75% difference‚Äîsignificant enough to base strategy on.

üí° Why This Matters (from a BA perspective):

When I present this to stakeholders, they ask: "Is this correlation or causation? Do diverse products retain customers, or do loyal customers naturally buy more products?" The answer is probably both, but the business implication is the same‚Äîwe have a leakage point at the 1-2 product customers.

‚úÖ What I Recommended:
  • Target customers stuck at 1-2 products for cross-sell campaigns
  • Estimate shows we could move 18% of this segment to 3+ products
  • If successful, would improve overall retention by ~2-3 percentage points
‚ö†Ô∏è Deeper Issue: Engagement Matters More Than I Thought ‚ñº

After presenting the cross-sell idea, I kept digging. The product diversity finding was useful, but I noticed something else that concerned me more. When I looked at inactive customers, the churn rate was much higher. This is where the real risk sits. 

-- What happens when customers go quiet?
SELECT 
    Months_Inactive_12_mon,
    COUNT(*) as customers,
    COUNT(*) FILTER (WHERE Attrition_Flag = 'Attrited Customer') as churned,
    ROUND(100.0 * COUNT(*) FILTER (WHERE Attrition_Flag = 'Attrited Customer') / COUNT(*), 1) as churn_rate
FROM credit_card_customers
GROUP BY Months_Inactive_12_mon
ORDER BY Months_Inactive_12_mon DESC;

This was eye-opening. The churn rate climbs sharply after 3-4 months of no activity. I combined this with contact frequency. 

-- Risk scoring: combining inactivity + low interaction
SELECT 
    CASE 
        WHEN Months_Inactive_12_mon >= 4 AND Contacts_Count_12_mon = 0 THEN 'Critical (Silent & Inactive)'
        WHEN Months_Inactive_12_mon >= 3 AND Total_Trans_Ct < 15 THEN 'High Risk (Quiet)'
        WHEN Months_Inactive_12_mon >= 2 THEN 'Medium Risk (Slowing Down)'
        ELSE 'Active'
    END as engagement_risk,
    COUNT(*) as customer_count,
    ROUND(100.0 * COUNT(*) FILTER (WHERE Attrition_Flag = 'Attrited Customer') / COUNT(*), 1) as actual_churn_rate,
    ROUND(AVG(Total_Trans_Amt), 0) as avg_annual_spend
FROM credit_card_customers
GROUP BY engagement_risk
ORDER BY actual_churn_rate DESC;
üéØ Key Insight:

The "Critical" segment (inactive + no contact) shows 60-70% churn rate. This group is basically already churned psychologically‚Äîthey just haven't formally left. We have maybe a 2-month window to intervene before they're gone.

üí° Business Impact:

This finding shifted the conversation. Instead of "let's do a cross-sell campaign," it became "we need an early warning system." The cross-sell strategy is fine for stable customers, but for at-risk customers, we need win-back tactics first.

‚úÖ Revised Strategy:
  • Flag customers entering "Medium Risk" at 2-month inactivity mark
  • Trigger automated outreach: special offer, reduced fees, something to re-engage
  • If successful in re-engaging them, THEN pitch cross-sell
  • Expected win-back rate: 35-45% if contacted promptly (vs near 0% after 6 months)
üí∞ Following the Money: Who's Spending Less? ‚ñº

In my experience as a BI analyst, I always follow revenue impact. Some churn signals matter because of volume, others because of high-value customers leaving. I wanted to identify which customers are most valuable to protect. 

-- Which customers are declining in spending?
SELECT 
    CASE 
        WHEN Total_Amt_Chng_Q4_Q1 < -0.10 AND Total_Ct_Chng_Q4_Q1 < -0.10 THEN 'Both declining'
        WHEN Total_Amt_Chng_Q4_Q1 < -0.10 THEN 'Spending down'
        WHEN Total_Ct_Chng_Q4_Q1 < -0.10 THEN 'Activity down'
        ELSE 'Stable/growing'
    END as spending_trend,
    COUNT(*) as customers,
    ROUND(AVG(Total_Trans_Amt), 0) as avg_annual_value,
    ROUND(100.0 * COUNT(*) FILTER (WHERE Attrition_Flag = 'Attrited Customer') / COUNT(*), 1) as churn_rate
FROM credit_card_customers
WHERE Attrition_Flag = 'Existing Customer'
GROUP BY spending_trend
ORDER BY churn_rate DESC;

This helped prioritize. Declining spending is a churn precursor‚Äîthe customer is already pulling back.

üéØ What I Learned:

Customers in "both declining" category are 80% likely to churn. But here's what's important: if we catch them in month 1-2 of decline (when it's still just -10%), we can intervene. By month 4-5 of decline, recovery is nearly impossible.

üí° Implementation Note:

In my BI role, I designed a simple alert logic: flag any customer with negative QoQ spending change > -10%. Pass those to the relationship management team monthly. This is actionable and easy to automate in Power BI.

‚úÖ Next Step:
  • Create monthly QoQ spending trend report for relationship managers
  • Combine with engagement score for prioritization
  • Focus retention efforts on "both declining" segment (highest ROI)
üéØ Integration: The Real At-Risk Customers ‚ñº

This is where BA thinking comes in. I have three risk factors (low product diversity, inactivity, declining spend). Rather than treat them separately, I weighted them to identify who needs immediate attention. 

-- Risk scoring: combining multiple signals
SELECT 
    CLIENTNUM,
    Total_Trans_Amt as annual_value,
    Months_Inactive_12_mon,
    Contacts_Count_12_mon,
    Total_Relationship_Count,
    CASE 
        WHEN Months_Inactive_12_mon >= 4 AND Contacts_Count_12_mon = 0 AND Total_Relationship_Count <= 2 THEN 'Critical'
        WHEN Months_Inactive_12_mon >= 3 AND Total_Trans_Ct < 15 THEN 'High'
        WHEN Months_Inactive_12_mon >= 2 OR Total_Amt_Chng_Q4_Q1 < -0.10 THEN 'Medium'
        ELSE 'Low'
    END as risk_level,
    Attrition_Flag
FROM credit_card_customers
WHERE Attrition_Flag = 'Existing Customer'
ORDER BY risk_level, Total_Trans_Amt DESC;

The top of this list‚Äî"Critical" customers with high annual value‚Äîis where the attention should be.

üéØ The Real Opportunity:

Found ~300-400 customers in "Critical" state. These are inactive, barely contacted, low product diversity, and historically high spenders. Loss of these customers would be material. But they're also most recoverable‚Äîthey haven't left yet.

üí° My Recommendation to Stakeholders:

Don't try to save everyone at once. Start with the 50 most valuable critical customers. Have relationship managers reach out personally, understand why they're silent, and offer something meaningful (fee waiver, better rates, new product fit). Measure recovery rate on this small cohort, then scale if it works.

‚úÖ Implementation Plan:
  • Week 1-2: RM team contacts top 50 critical customers directly (not email, phone)
  • Week 3-4: Analyze what worked/didn't. Adjust messaging.
  • Month 2: Scale to remaining 250-350 critical customers with learnings
  • Ongoing: Monthly monitoring of "Medium" tier to catch before they become "Critical"

üìã Summary of Learnings

  • Product diversity matters (13.75% retention gap), but it's a long-term play
  • Engagement is the early warning (2-month inactivity window is critical)
  • Spend trends tell you WHO to save first (declining customers are at-risk)
  • Combining signals works better than single metrics (risk levels give better prioritization)
  • Timing matters (month 2 of inactivity vs month 6 = completely different recovery rates)
